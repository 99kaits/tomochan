This is new /Δ/, also addressable as /diy/, the projects board. The intent is that this board will be used to share and discuss physical building projects and other creations not suitable on other boards, such as cooking, automobile work, structural modifications to buildings, metal working, and circuitry design.

Discussion of computer working or electrical design and modification may be considered more appropriate on /Ω/ or /λ/; a thread in each or any of these boards for this is reasonable and allowed.

Creation of artwork, including music, games, visuals, and literature will belong in the /culture/ overboard, rather than this board. It's important to make certain no other board commands a subset of a project type before starting a thread here.

Do not give advice that is misleadingly dangerous or otherwise purposefully misleadingly harmful on this board. Failure to comply will result in a public ban indicating such or deletion.

If a large topic, such as cooking specific categories of food, already has a thread, try to use that rather than starting a new thread, if reasonable; starting a long-term project is an example of a topic that reasonably warrants its own thread. In particular, it's not reasonable for a particular medium to overwhelm the others across the board.

>>12698
>dont know where to start
If you actually care, if you're in that situation, the best bet is probably to move someplace better.

Short of that you want to spend the bulk of your time away from the probable targets. If you have to work close to a target maybe you can't change that, but hopefully your house can be a relatively safe location. If you can move in town, consider a place that is shielded by terrain from probable targets. Even if you're not sure where a bomb will blow, its better to be close to a hill that might block a considerable fraction of line of site to possible detonation points. There are a lot of points to consider, police stations often are good to live by because the buildings in their vicinity often get on the same special power grid which means you'll be first to get power restored and receive other special infrastructure attention, for instance.

You'll want to harden your house. Windows are bad for a lot of reasons. I imagine installing plastic windows might be better for anti shatter reasons, but you could also likely install plastic screens on the inside.

Another point to be concerned about is the immediate aftermath. You'll want food, water, supplies to last at least a few weeks. A lot of the danger of fallout is escapeable if you avoid contact with dust. Get air filters, plastic sheeting, and a staple gun so you can cover windows (which may have shattered) and doors during this critical time.

You want to keep a respirator in your car, and if possible a secondary mode of transport like a folding bike that you can pull out if either your car is disabled, or roads are impassible. Get robust tires ( I have good experience with kevlar tires), spare inner tubes, and a pump. When you get home be prepared to discard your clothes and thoroughly clean yourself even without power or running water, and you will want this cleaning solution accessible near your house's entrance at all times. Make sure you know how to get around even with landmarks altered.

After that typical prepper plans apply.

A few years ago I read this book, A Small Farm Future. It is more of a technical and data driven breakdown of what the author believes will be the structure of food production systems after the collapse of global supply chains and megacorporations. He believes that things will become more locally focused and less carbon energy-dependant not only because it's a common goal, but because of nessecity. More like "it's going to happen, how should we manuver as it happens".

to be honest it's not the best book I've read, I think it takes a long time to get to its main points. The main ideas of each chapter *could* be pretty easily communicated in like 3 or 4 bullet points (though I suppose that's true of any publication? Just the feeling I got)

In relation to permaculture, the best point he argues is the difference in harvesting from Skims versus Stores.

A store of a good is an output that consumes the good and the producing method at the same time.

Skimming would be taking from an output that is continuously generated and renewable, or in some form of ongoing system or lifecycle.

For example: a chicken has skims in the form of eggs, or stores in the form of meat. A cow has skims of milk (lol) and stores of meat.

An example other than food, he argues that a deposit of coal or oil is a store, while solar/wind are a skim.

Permaculture systems are more likely to be this type of system, like a food forest.
